# The Keeper

For thirty-one winters Aldous had kept the light, and in all that time he had
never once let it fail. The lamp was a living thing to him — it breathed oil,
it turned on its brass heart, and it asked, in return, only that a man be there
at dusk to wake it.[^lamp]

The village below thought him mad, or holy, or simply old. He did not mind.
Madness and holiness were only two names for the same long attention, and old
age was the price of paying it.[^attention] What the village could not see was
that the sea kept its own accounts, and the light was how a keeper settled them.

Tonight the glass was cold under his palm. Far out, past the shoals, something
moved that was not weather — a slow, deliberate dark, coming in against the tide.

[^lamp]: A first-order Fresnel lens, ground in Paris, could throw a beam
nineteen miles. Aldous had polished this one until he knew every prism by touch.
[^attention]: "The keeper does not watch the sea," his father had told him.
"He watches the dark, and lets the sea watch him."
